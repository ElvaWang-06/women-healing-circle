About the Circle

Why we gather

Women's bodies and mental health have long been underrepresented in research and care. The Gender Pain Gap, the historical exclusion of women from clinical research, and the "Weathering" hypothesis — which links chronic exposure to structural racism with premature health decline — are not abstractions. They are lived realities, especially for women of color.

"Healing is collective, embodied, and culturally rooted. This is not performance — it is presence."

The Women's Health and Healing Circle is a community-based arts and public health initiative grounded in somatic movement, storytelling, and shared care. We create accessible, adaptable sessions designed around each participant's comfort and agency — not outcome, not achievement.

This project was built with and for women of color in Cambridge, MA, in partnership with the Harvard Graduate School of Education and the Cambridge Public Health Department.

What We Do

The practices

Each session is an invitation — not an instruction. Come as you are.

01

Somatic Movement & Breathwork

Gentle, intentional movement sequences that help you reconnect with your body. Guided breathwork, slow stretching, and grounding exercises to settle the nervous system. No prior experience needed — only willingness to be present.

02

Meditation & Grounding

Accessible yoga-inspired meditation and rhythm-based movement. Sessions create space for stillness, self-awareness, and interoceptive connection — a return to the body as a place of knowing, not judgment.

03

Embodied Storytelling & Theatre

Transform personal experiences into shared narratives. Through structured improvisation, mirroring exercises, and collaborative performance, women tell stories through gesture, voice, and movement — on their own terms.

04

Collaborative Movement Exercises

Pairs and group exercises that build trust and connection. Guided improvisation focused on emotional expression through physical presence. These sessions cultivate collective care and embodied resilience together.

The Team

Madiha Musarrat

Dance · Theatre · Community Facilitation

A trained Kathak dancer and theatre artist, Madiha has performed on national radio and television in India. She is the founder of Nisa Collective in New Delhi — a community of women who gather to express, reflect, and connect. She believes deeply in the creation of safe spaces where women can create, spiritually and artistically.

Elva

Visual Art · Embodied Painting · Co-creation

Elva's paintings explore femininity as a lived, embodied experience — not a fixed identity. Her figures shift between individual and collective bodies, expressing seduction, maternity, longing, and melancholy. Grounded in memory and sensation, her work reflects how health and care are under-experienced in women's everyday lives.

Naolin

Research · Community Outreach · Arts Education

Naolin brings experience as a director of research and community outreach liaison. Her paintings center the female body, uplifting and honoring women of color through themes of identity and strength. She has partnered with ARCK Boston to expand creative opportunity and confidence in youth, connecting art, education, and community impact.

Folly

Embodied Learning · Africana History · Somatic Practice

A historian, philosopher, and educator, Folly integrates somatic movement with creative practice as a source of holistic healing. He has worked with youths, adults, and incarcerated people across trauma-processing and emotional regulation. His research in Africana history and cultural preservation grounds the Circle's commitment to honoring cultural memory and storytelling traditions.

Our Process

Co-created,
participant-centered

We don't deliver programming to a community. We build it with one. Participants shape themes, movement exercises, and storytelling practices based on their own lived experiences.

  • Consent first. Nothing is required. All documentation and participation is voluntary and shaped by community agreement.
  • Shared authorship. Participants' voices, perspectives, and stories belong to them — not the project.
  • Gradual trust. We build relationships slowly, through repeated gathering and mutual care.
  • Cultural responsiveness. Programming adapts to the community, not the other way around.
